DRIP-seq (DRIP-sequencing) is a technology for genome-wide profiling of a type of DNA-RNA hybrid called "R-loop"[1]. DRIP-seq utilizes a sequence-independent but structure specific antibody for DNA-RNA immunoprecipitation (DRIP) to capture R-loops for massively parallel DNA sequencing[1].

Introduction[edit]

R-loops are three-stranded nucleic acid structures predominantly formed in cytosine-rich DNA regions during transcription. They are composed of a DNA-RNA hybrid duplex and a displaced single stranded DNA (ssDNA)[2]. R-loops are found in a broad range of species ranging from bacteria to mammals and are involved in gene expression and immunoglobulin class switching[1][3][4]. They are preferentially localized at CpG island promoters in human cells and highly transcribed regions in wild-type yeast[1][3].

Under abnormal conditions, namely elevated production of DNA-RNA hybrids, R-loops can cause genome instability by exposing single-stranded DNA to endogenous damages exerted by the action of enzymes such as AID and APOBEC, or overexposure to chemically reactive species[4]. Thus, the knowledge of where and in what circumstances R-loops are formed across the genome are crucial information for the better understanding of genome instability and their consequences for the cell. R-loop characterization was initially limited to locus specific approaches[5]. However, upon the arrival of massive parallel sequencing technologies and thereafter derivatives like DRIP-seq, the possibility to investigate entire genomes for R-loops opened up.

DRIP-seq relies on the high specificity and affinity the S9.6 monoclonal antibody (mAb) towards DNA-RNA hybrids of various lengths. S9.6 mAb was first discovered and characterized in 1986 and is currently used for the selective immunoprecipitation of R-loops[6]. Since then it was used in diverse immunoprecipitation methods for R-loop characterization[1][3][7][8]. DRIP-seq is in essence a variant of ChIP-sequencing where R-loop fragments are the main immunoprecipitated material. After purification, washing and elution of the R-loop-containing nucleic acids, the fragments are further sheared for size selection and ligated to adapters for high-throughput sequencing.

Uses and Current Research[edit]

DRIP-seq was first used for genome-wide profiling of R-loop in human, which showed widespread R-loop formation at CpG island promoters[1]. Particularly, the researchers found that R-loop formation is associated with the unmethylated state of CpG islands.

It was later used to profile R-loop formation at transcription start and termination sites in human pluripotent Ntera2 cells[7]. In this study, the researchers revealed that R-loops on 3' ends of genes may be correlated with transcription termination.

Workflow of DRIP-seq[edit]

Workflow of DRIP-seq

Genomic DNA extraction[edit]

First, genomic DNA (gDNA) is extracted from cells of interest by proteinase K treatment followed by phenol-chloroform extraction and ethanol precipitation. Additional zymolyase digestion is necessary for yeast cells to remove the cell wall prior to proteinase K treatment. gDNA can also be extracted with a variety of other methods, such as column-based methods.

Genomic DNA fragmentation[edit]

gDNA is treated with S1 nuclease to remove undesired ssDNA and RNA, followed by ethanol precipitation to remove the S1 nuclease. Then, gDNA is fragmented with restriction endonuclease, yielding double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) fragments of different sizes. Alternatively, gDNA fragments can be generated by sonication.

Immunoprecipitation[edit]

Fragmented gDNA is incubated with the DNA-RNA structure-specific S9.6 antibody. This step is unique for the DRIP-seq protocol, since it entirely relies on the high specificity and affinity of the S9.6 monoclonal antibody for DNA-RNA hybrids. The antibody will recognize and bind these regions dispersed across the genome and will be used for immunoprecipitation. The S9.6 antibodies are bound to magnetic beads by interacting with specific ligands (i.e. protein A or protein G) on the surface of the beads. Thus, the DNA-RNA containing fragments will bind to the beads by means of the antibody.

Elution[edit]

The magnetic beads are washed to remove any gDNA not bound to the beads by a series of washes and DNA-RNA hybrids are recovered by elution. To remove the antibody bound to the nucleic acid hybrids, proteinase K treatment is performed followed by phenol-chloroform extraction and ethanol precipitation. This results in the isolation of purified DNA-RNA hybrids of different sizes.

Sequencing[edit]

For massive parallel sequencing of these fragments, the immunoprecipitated material is sonicated, size selected and ligated to barcoded oligonucleotide adaptors for cluster enrichment and sequencing.

Computational Analysis[edit]

To detect sites of R-loop formation, the hundreds of millions of sequencing reads from DRIP-seq are first aligned to a reference genome with a short-read sequence aligner, then peak calling methods designed for ChIP-seq can be used to evaluate DRIP signals[1]. If different cocktails of restriction enzymes were used for different DRIP-seq experiments of the same sample, consensus DRIP-seq peaks are called[7]. Typically, peaks are compared against those from a corresponding RNase H1-treated sample, which serves as an input control[1][7].

Limitations[edit]

Typical short-read sequencing platforms would produce uneven read coverage in GC-rich regions. Sequencing long R-loops might pose a challenge because R-loops are predominantly formed in cytosine-rich DNA regions. Moreover, GC-rich regions tend to have low complexity by nature, which is difficult for short read aligners to produce unique alignments.

Other R-loop Profiling Methods[edit]

Although several other methods for the analysis and profiling of R-loop formation exist[5][9][10][11][12][13][14][15][16], only few provide coverage and robustness at the genome-wide scale [1][3].

  • Non-denaturing bisulfite modification and sequencing: This method comprises of bisulfite treatment followed by sequencing and relies on the mutagenic effect of sodium bisulfite on ssDNA. Although this method is primarily used to localize specific CpG island promoters [1], it was used to detect R-loops at a minor scale[5] and other ssDNA fragile sites[17].
  • DNA:RNA In Vitro Enrichment (DRIVE-seq)[1]: This method follows very similar principles of DRIP-seq except for the use of MBP-RNASEH1 instead of the S9.6 mAb for R-loops recovery. MBP-RNASEH1 may provide a good alternative to S9.6 mAb when an additional capture assay is needed.
  • DNA:RNA immnunoprecipitation followed by hybridization on tiling microarray (DRIP-chip)[3]: This method also relies on the use of the S9.6 mAb. However, instead of entering into a sequencing pipeline, the immunoprecipitated material in DRIP-chip is hybridized to a microarray. The rapid obtention of the data is an advantage comparing to DRIP-seq. The number of probes on the chip microarrays and no DNA sequence information are the limiting factors of this technique.
